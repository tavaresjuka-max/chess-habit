## 1. De Groot, A. D. (1965/1978)
**Thought and Choice in Chess**
*Mouton & Co. (English translation, 1978; original Dutch, 1946)*
~5,631 citações | Google Scholar

- **Key finding**: Chess masters do not search deeper than amateurs — they perceive the board differently. Masters instantly "see" the promising moves because their perceptual system encodes the position in terms of meaningful patterns (chunks). This established chess as *the* model domain for expertise research.
- **Access**: PAYWALL (book). Reprint ISBN 978-90-5356-998-6 (Amsterdam University Press, 2008). Available in most university libraries.
- **Note**: **A** — Foundational to all chess cognition research. Directly informs mastery thresholds and why pattern quantity matters.
- **Covers**: `threshold-dominio`, `proporcao-revisao-vs-novo`

---

## 2. Chase, W. G., & Simon, H. A. (1973)
**Perception in Chess**
*Cognitive Psychology, 4*(1), 55–81
~8,654 citações | Google Scholar

- **Key finding**: Chess skill differences are explained by the number and size of stored "chunks" (familiar piece configurations). Masters store an estimated 10,000–100,000 chunks. When positions are randomized instead of game-like, the expert advantage vanishes — proving chunking, not raw memory, drives chess skill.
- **Access**: PAYWALL (Elsevier). Widely available on ResearchGate, Academia.edu, and Andy Matuschak's site (PDF).
- **Note**: **A** — Quantifies the "database" size needed for mastery. Core to any chunk-count-based mastery threshold model.
- **Covers**: `threshold-dominio`

---

## 3. Gobet, F., & Simon, H. A. (1996)
**Templates in Chess Memory: A Mechanism for Recalling Several Boards**
*Cognitive Psychology, 31*(1), 1–40
~1,037 citações | Google Scholar

- **Key finding**: Extends Chase & Simon's chunking theory into **template theory**: masters use configurable "templates" (~12 pieces with slots for variable information) rather than fixed 4–5 piece chunks. Templates explain how masters can recall multiple boards rapidly and maintain high performance even with randomized-but-structured positions.
- **Access**: PAYWALL (Elsevier). PDF available from Fernand Gobet's website at Brunel University (brunel.ac.uk).
- **Note**: **A** — Refines the chunk-size and template count model. Directly relevant to estimating how many patterns a learner needs at each ELO band.
- **Covers**: `threshold-dominio`

---

## 4. Bilalić, M., Langner, R., Erb, M., & Grodd, W. (2010)
**Mechanisms and Neural Basis of Object and Pattern Recognition: A Study with Chess Experts**
*Journal of Experimental Psychology: General, 139*(4), 728–742
~228 citações | Google Scholar

- **Key finding**: Chess experts recruit fusiform face area (FFA) and occipito-temporal cortex for holistic board pattern recognition — the same areas used for face recognition. Novices rely on piece-by-piece analysis. This demonstrates that chess pattern recognition is a genuine perceptual expertise, not just abstract reasoning, and that neural reorganization follows skill acquisition.
- **Access**: PAYWALL (APA PsycNet). Available via ResearchGate and PubMed Central (nih.gov).
- **Note**: **A** — Neural evidence that pattern recognition is perceptual, not analytical. Supports the argument that training should optimize perceptual fluency, not just explicit knowledge.
- **Covers**: `threshold-dominio`

## 5. Ericsson, K. A., Krampe, R. T., & Tesch-Römer, C. (1993)
**The Role of Deliberate Practice in the Acquisition of Expert Performance**
*Psychological Review, 100*(3), 363–406
~17,817 citações | Google Scholar

- **Key finding**: Individual differences in chess ELO are overwhelmingly predicted by hours of "deliberate practice" (solitary, effortful study aimed at improvement), not innate talent. The best violinists accumulated ~10,000 hours by age 20. Chess masters spent ~10,000–16,000 hours in solitary chess study. The study sets the theoretical framework for all deliberate practice research.
- **Access**: PAYWALL (APA). Widely available on ResearchGate, Academia.edu, and countless preprints.
- **Note**: **A** — Establishes deliberate practice as the prime driver of chess ELO. Core reference for any "hours → ELO" or study-prescription model.
- **Covers**: `proporcao-revisao-vs-novo`, chess ELO improvement meta-analysis

## 6. Macnamara, B. N., Hambrick, D. Z., & Oswald, F. L. (2014)
**Deliberate Practice and Performance in Music, Games, Sports, Education, and Professions: A Meta-Analysis**
*Psychological Science, 25*(8), 1608–1618
~1,086 citações | Google Scholar

- **Key finding**: Meta-analysis of 88 studies shows deliberate practice explains only **26% of variance in chess performance** (far less than Ericsson's ~80% claim). Domain matters: practice explains 26% for games (including chess), 21% for music, 18% for sports, <1% for professions. Other factors (starting age, working memory, intelligence) have larger effects than previously acknowledged.
- **Access**: PAYWALL (Sage). Widely available on ResearchGate and via Rice University repository (rice.edu).
- **Note**: **A** — Critical nuance to Ericsson: deliberate practice is *necessary but not sufficient*. Informs realistic expectations for training ROI and the role of non-practice factors.
- **Covers**: chess ELO improvement meta-analysis, `proporcao-revisao-vs-novo`

## 7. Cepeda, N. J., Pashler, H., Vul, E., Wixted, J. T., & Rohrer, D. (2006)
**Distributed Practice in Verbal Recall Tasks: A Review and Quantitative Synthesis**
*Psychological Bulletin, 132*(3), 354–380
~3,051 citações | Google Scholar

- **Key finding**: Meta-analysis of 317 experiments. Longer inter-study gaps (spacing) produce *dramatically* better long-term retention, but the optimal gap scales with the retention interval (RI): optimal gap ≈ 10–20% of RI. For a test 7 days away, study every 1–2 days; for a test 1 year away, study every 1–2 months. Massed practice ("cramming") consistently underperforms for any RI > 1 day.
- **Access**: PAYWALL (APA). PDF widely available via eScholarship (escholarship.org) and ed.gov.
- **Note**: **A** — The canonical spacing effect paper. Provides the quantitative formula for calculating review intervals based on desired retention horizon.
- **Covers**: `intervalos-repeticao-espacada`

## 8. Bahrick, H. P., Bahrick, L. E., Bahrick, A. S., & Bahrick, P. E. (1993)
**Maintenance of Foreign Language Vocabulary and the Spacing Effect**
*Psychological Science, 4*(5), 316–321
~563 citações | Google Scholar

- **Key finding**: Over a 5-year retention interval, vocabulary learned with 56-day spacing between practice sessions showed far superior retention compared to 13-day spacing. The benefit of wider spacing compounds over time — at 5 years, 56-day group recalled ~2x more. The optimal spacing gap increases with the desired duration of retention.
- **Access**: PAYWALL (Sage). Available via ResearchGate and Academia.edu.
- **Note**: **A** — Provides empirical anchoring for "how long should the interval be" questions. Complements Cepeda et al. (2006) with very long-term data (years, not days).
- **Covers**: `intervalos-repeticao-espacada`

## 9. Pavlik, P. I., & Anderson, J. R. (2008)
**Using a Model to Compute the Optimal Schedule of Practice**
*Journal of Experimental Psychology: Applied, 14*(2), 101–117
~358 citações | Google Scholar

- **Key finding**: An ACT-R cognitive model computes individualized optimal practice schedules. Key insight: easier items benefit from expanding intervals (1→3→7 days), while harder items benefit from equal spacing. The model adapts scheduling based on the learner's error rate — items frequently answered correctly get longer intervals; items with errors get compressed intervals. The model outperformed fixed schedules and maximal-spacing heuristics.
- **Access**: PAYWALL (APA). Preprint PDF available at cmu.edu.
- **Note**: **A** — Most directly applicable to building an adaptive spaced repetition engine for chess. Provides the algorithmic logic for adjusting intervals by difficulty.
- **Covers**: `intervalos-repeticao-espacada`, `threshold-dominio` (difficulty-adaptive thresholds)

## 10. Kornell, N., & Bjork, R. A. (2008)
**Learning Concepts and Categories: Is Spacing the "Enemy of Induction"?**
*Psychological Science, 19*(6), 585–592
~1,095 citações | Google Scholar

- **Key finding**: When learners must *induce* categories (learn to classify paintings by artist), interleaving different artists' paintings during study dramatically improved test performance (84% vs 37% for blocked). However, pure spacing within a category — without interleaving across categories — actually *hurt* induction. The mechanism: interleaving promotes **discrimination** (seeing what makes categories different), which is the essence of induction. Blocking creates fluency illusions.
- **Access**: PAYWALL (Sage). Widely available on ResearchGate and Williams College site.
- **Note**: **A** — Foundational for interleaving vs. blocking. Chess pattern recognition is fundamentally category induction (is this a Sicilian or a Caro-Kann? Is this a tactical motif or not?).
- **Covers**: `interleaving-sessao`, `proporcao-revisao-vs-novo`

## 11. Rohrer, D. (2012)
**Interleaving Helps Students Distinguish Among Similar Concepts**
*Educational Psychology Review, 24*(3), 355–367
~371 citações | Google Scholar

- **Key finding**: In mathematics, interleaved practice (mixing problem types) improved test scores by ~43% compared to blocked practice (doing all of one type, then all of another). The benefit is specifically for *discrimination* — knowing which strategy to apply, not just how to apply it. Blocked practice gave students and teachers illusion of mastery because performance was high during practice but collapsed on the test. Interleaving feels harder but produces durable, transferable learning.
- **Access**: FREE. PDF available via ERIC/ed.gov.
- **Note**: **A** — Direct analogue to chess: mixing tactical motifs (forks, pins, discovered attacks) in one session builds discrimination of which motif applies. Provides concrete "how many themes per session" guidance (at least 2–3, interleaved).
- **Covers**: `interleaving-sessao`

## 12. Soderstrom, N. C., & Bjork, R. A. (2015)
**Learning Versus Performance: An Integrative Review**
*Perspectives on Psychological Science, 10*(2), 176–199
~1,100+ citações | Google Scholar

- **Key finding**: Conditions that maximize *performance* during practice (blocked scheduling, constant conditions, immediate feedback, massed repetition) often minimize *learning* (measured by retention and transfer). Conversely, "desirable difficulties" — spacing, interleaving, variability, reduced feedback, testing — depress performance during acquisition but enhance long-term learning. Learners and instructors consistently misinterpret short-term performance as evidence of learning.
- **Access**: PAYWALL (Sage). Available via ResearchGate and many institutional repositories.
- **Note**: **A** — Critical framework for interpreting *any* practice data. Explains why blocked practice feels good but interleaving works. Informs mastery threshold design: momentary % correct ≠ learned status.
- **Covers**: `threshold-dominio`, `interleaving-sessao`

## Key Observations

1. **No paper defines a precise "% correct → mastered" threshold for chess patterns.** The closest analogue is Pavlik & Anderson (2008), which uses ACT-R activation decay to adapt intervals — a model-based approach rather than a fixed % cutoff. Practical guidance: ~80–90% correct with expanding intervals is the operational definition used in spaced repetition systems (SM2 algorithm), but this is engineering wisdom, not peer-reviewed chess research.

2. **No paper directly studies "how many chess themes per session."** Rohrer (2012) on math and Kornell & Bjork (2008) on art styles are the closest proxies. Both support interleaving 2+ categories per session. Extrapolating to chess: 2–3 tactical themes interleaved per session, with discrimination (which motif?) being the training target.

3. **The spacing formula is well-established:** Cepeda et al. (2006) provides gap ≈ 10–20% of retention interval. For a user wanting 30-day retention, review every 3–6 days. For 1-year retention, review every 1–2 months.

4. **The deliberate practice debate is nuanced:** Ericsson (1993) claims ~80% of chess ELO variance explained by practice; Macnamara (2014) meta-analysis finds only 26%. Both sides agree practice matters — they disagree on magnitude. For a personal training tool, the practical implication is: maximize quality and quantity of practice, but do not expect linear ELO gains from hours alone.

## Limitations

- No search returned papers on `mastery threshold` combined with `percent correct` and `spaced repetition` as an exact match — this exact phrase does not appear to be a named construct in the literature.
- Chess.com/Lichess training data (e.g., Puzzle Storm, Puzzle Racer) is not peer-reviewed. Any in-app mastery threshold should be calibrated from user data, not from the literature.
- The interleaving literature comes predominantly from mathematics and perceptual categorization, not chess specifically. Direct chess-interleaving studies were not found in these searches.
